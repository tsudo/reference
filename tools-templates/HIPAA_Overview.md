# What is HIPAA?

The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) was enacted to improve efficiency and effectiveness of the healthcare system and to set national standards for code sets, health identifiers, and security.

Four primary rules pertaining to HIPAA:

* Privacy
* Security
* Breach Notification
* Enforcement

## Terms and Acronyms

* HIPAA – Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, [Public Law 104-191](https://www.govinfo.gov/app/details/PLAW-104publ191)
* HITECH - Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health, [Public Law 111-5 Sec XIII](https://www.govinfo.gov/app/details/PLAW-111publ5), Subtitle D
* HHS – US Department of Health and Human Services (Regulatory Agency)
* OCR – Office of Civil Rights (Enforcement Agency)
* BAA – Business Associate Agreement
* PHI – Protected Health Information (see below)
* ePHI – Electronic PHI

### Protected Health Information (PHI)

Protected health information (PHI) is any information in the medical record or designated record set that can be used to identify an individual and that was created, used, or disclosed in the course of providing a health care service such as diagnosis or treatment.

PHI is PII that can also be used to connect a patient to past, present, or future physical or mental health care or payments. PHI is protected by HIPAA and cannot be shared or disclosed to unauthorized individuals. Covered entities are responsible for protecting all forms of PHI: spoken, written (paper), or electronic.

Personally Identifiable Information (PII)

* Name
* Address
* DOB / SSN

Protected Health Information (PHI)

* Diagnosis / Procedure
* Physician Name
* DOS

*\*List of the 18 PHI identifiers is located in Appendix A*

# HIPAA Rules

## HIPAA Privacy Rule

The HIPAA Privacy Rule pertains to covered entities and business associates.

Covered entity:

* Health Care Provider
* Health Plan
* Health Care Clearinghouse

Business associate:

* A company whom the covered entity has engaged with to help carry out its health care activities

Permitted Uses and Disclosures:

A Covered Entity is permitted, but not required, to use and disclose protected health information, without an individual’s authorization, for the following purposes or situations:

* To the individual (unless required for access or accounting of disclosures)
* Treatment, payment, and health care operations
* Opportunity to agree or object
* Incident to an otherwise permitted use and disclosure
* Public Interest and benefit activities
* Limited data set

\*Always use the MINIMUM NECESSARY rule\*

## HIPAA Security Rule

Sets national standards for electronic protected health information (ePHI).

* EHR – Electronic Health Record
* CPOE – Computerized Physician Order Entry
* Radiology, lab, & pharmacy systems

The general rules for HIPAA Security state covered entities are to:

1. Maintain reasonable and *appropriate* safeguards for protecting ePHI including:
   1. Administrative
   2. Technical
   3. Physical
2. Maintain confidentiality, integrity, and availability of ePHI
3. Identify and protect against reasonably anticipated threats to security or integrity of ePHI
4. Protect against identified threats and ensure compliance by the workforce

## Breach Notification Rule

A covered entity is required to notify the Secretary of Health and Human Services upon a breach of unsecured protected health information.

#### Breaches affecting fewer than 500 individuals:

* Notify within 60 days of the end of the calendar year the breach was discovered
* Can report at one time but on separate notice per incident

#### Breaches affecting more than 500 individuals:

* Notify without *unreasonable* delay and no later than 60 calendar days from the discovery of the breach
* Notify the breach to prominent media outlets within the same timeframe

In both situations the covered entity is responsible for notifying the affected individuals.

Examples of common breaches:

* Unauthorized person using your credentials
* Lab results to the wrong patient
* Stolen laptop or USB device
* Business email compromise

Following a notification to HHS, the Office for Civil Rights (OCR), who is responsible for enforcing HIPAA, will conduct an audit. This audit will review what security measures were in place to mitigate the risk of a breach, will review actions taken to investigate the breach, and actions taken in response to the breach.

## Enforcement Rule

The HITECH (Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health) Act was enacted in February 2009 to enforce and establish:

* Categories of violations
* Corresponding tiers of penalties
* Set maximum penalty for violations of an identical provision

The Office for Civil Rights (OCR) is responsible for investigating complaints, conducting compliance review audits, and performing education and outreach.

# Breach Costs and Fines

## OCR Enforcement

Fines for HIPAA violations vary based on severity *and* perceived negligence found within the organization and range from $100 per violation to $50,000 per violation with a maximum penalty of $1.5 million per year per violation. ([HHS Civil Monetary Penalties for HIPAA Violations adjusted for inflation](https://www.hipaajournal.com/hhs-increases-civil-monetary-penalties-for-hipaa-violations-2019-inflation/))

Notifications to HHS that affect more than 500 individuals are placed on the ‘[Wall of Shame](https://ocrportal.hhs.gov/ocr/breach/breach_report.jsf)’ on the OCR portal.

### Breaches and Notable Fines

Here are some recent examples of breaches, violations, and resulting fines and penalties. Note that most fines are not for the breach occurring, but rather lack of compliance.

* [Orthopedic Clinic](https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/2020/09/21/orthopedic-clinic-pays-1.5-million-to-settle-systemic-noncompliance-with-hipaa-rules.html) – Attacker used vendor’s credentials. CE failed to conduct risk management, policies, BAAs or training. Fine: **$1.5M** (Complete Failure)
* [Sentara Hospital System](https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/2019/11/27/ocr-secures-2.175-million-dollars-hipaa-settlement-breach-notification-and-privacy-rules.html) – CE failed to report breach, even after being specifically advised by OCR. Failed to have BAA. Fine: **$2.175M** – (Don’t be dumb, if OCR makes suggestion, follow it)
* [University Medical System](https://www.hhs.gov/about/news/2019/11/05/failure-to-encrypt-mobile-devices-leads-to-3-million-dollar-hipaa-settlement.html) – Failed to do risk management and failed to encrypt mobile devices. Previously had this problem in 2010. Fine **$3M**. – (Don’t repeat mistakes)

## auditing and compliance

### Real World Insight

It is not *IF* you get audited it is *WHEN* you get audited. 1 in 300-500 healthcare entities will be audited.

* HHS, by law, must investigate ALL HIPAA violation complaints
* A change in 2015 allows the government to do random HIPAA audits
* A 79% failure rate in MIPS attestations and CMS will report you to OCR and trigger an audit

Policies and Procedures must be updated/reviewed annually, and staff must review and sign annually.

Employees can be held personally liable if they participated in a negligent act.

*Example of employee accessing their boyfriends mothers’ records but the entity was not held liable because they had record of the employee reading and signing that they understand the PnP and HIPAA training with date and time.*

# Appendix A: PHI Defined

Protected health information (PHI) is any information in the medical record or designated record set that can be used to identify an individual and that was created, used, or disclosed in the course of providing a health care service such as diagnosis or treatment.

1. Names;
2. All geographical subdivisions smaller than a State, including street address, city, county, precinct, and zip code.
3. All elements of dates (except year) for dates directly related to an individual, including: Birth date, admission date, discharge date, and date of death.
4. Phone numbers;
5. Fax numbers;
6. Electronic mail addresses;
7. Social Security numbers;
8. Medical record numbers;
9. Health plan beneficiary numbers;
10. Account numbers;
11. Certificate/license numbers;
12. Vehicle identifiers and serial numbers, including license plate numbers;
13. Device identifiers and serial numbers;
14. Web Universal Resource Locators (URLs);
15. Internet Protocol (IP) address numbers;
16. Biometric identifiers, including finger and voice prints;
17. Full face photographic images and any comparable images; and
18. Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code (note this does not mean the unique code assigned by the investigator to code the data)

There are also additional standards and criteria to protect individual's privacy from re-identification. Any code used to replace the identifiers in datasets cannot be derived from any information related to the individual and the master codes, nor can the method to derive the codes be disclosed. For example, a subject's initials cannot be used to code their data because the initials are derived from their name. Additionally, the researcher must not have actual knowledge that the research subject could be re-identified from the remaining identifiers in the PHI used in the research study. In other words, the information would still be considered identifiable is there was a way to identify the individual even though all of the 18 identifiers were removed.
